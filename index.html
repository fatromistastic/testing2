Thoughts about art and my music


Every composer I know is haunted by the question ‘What sort of music do you write?’
Clicking the question below randomly retrieves a thought I’ve had about my music, or art in general, that I think is meaningful and shares something about my music and why I make art. Sometimes it will simply attempt to answer the question 'what is your music about?' and 'why do you make it?'. Some of the answers take themselves too seriously, and others probably not seriously enough. I hope the experience is balanced overall by these systemic miscalculations.

This personal experiment is a kind of ‘meaning engine’ (and, I think, more fun than an artist’s statement or manifesto). I think of meaning as, simultaneously (and contradictorily), constructed and intrinsic to being in the world.

This ‘meaning engine’ generates its meaning by randomly selecting from disparate (but related) thoughts, thought over a long period of time, in a way that is both more comprehensive, and random, than my brain would be capable of doing on its own, on command, and less fortified and coherent than an artist’s statement probably should be.

Rather than dwelling on my failure (and perhaps disinclination) to have a good answer to a perfectly reasonable question, this collates and offers more good answers than anyone would ever need, want, or ever take on board and respond to - an overwhelming set of responses to an overwhelming question.

To me, asking ‘what sort of music do you write?’ is as abstract and baffling as asking someone who is a chef ‘what flavour is your cooking’? Maybe they would have an answer, but even if they did, if it’s not something with a generally accepted or understood shared meaning, then one is stuck in the void regardless.

As I wrote things down, over many years, I slowly realised that part of the problem was not a lack of satisfactory answers but the information age dilemma: so many answers that it was impossible to distil any one personal answer on demand without a substantial loss of detail, and insight. And as I started answering the one question, I started finding answers to entirely new questions, too.

So I decided to build a generative engine that, when asked, provides one of the many possible answers I might prefer about my work or art in general. This is that engine - a distillation, without loss, of so many thoughts about why I do what I do and what I think is important, and not important, about it.

I don’t think a single sentence or two captures what I do and why I do it, so I’ve collated far too many sentences instead, and a mechanism for randomly navigating them. Now the answer does not rest on anything singular but instead on the individual networks of possibility created each time you click.
